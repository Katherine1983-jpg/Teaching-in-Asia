Tips when moving with a family

Breathing Space

My husband and I decided to take the first holiday we’d had in years. We couldn’t afford to, but we needed time to consider our future and at home the routine we’d found ourselves in didn’t allow for clear, level–headed thinking. We were both working in full-time teaching jobs, in managerial posts which required a lot of our time and drained our emotions. Taking a break from the usual routine was a good idea and allowed us to evaluate our situation. It was really only on this trip to a snowy cabin in Wales that we realised were we ready for an adventure abroad. If you are unsure about whether teaching in a different country is the right path for you, taking some time to clear your head can be a great start.

Push Factors

When I decided to apply, the push factors were likely be familiar to many teachers, especially those with a young family: feeling totally exhausted at the end of another week teaching in a school with a class of 30+ students. With having three young children at home, money was tight. Nights out were few and far between. When the weekend did come round, work pervaded - our minds would wander, thinking about the combative meeting just around the corner with an angry parent or overbearing pedant on the leadership team. The soothing light at the end of the tunnel is just a freight train coming your way; don’t worry, when you’re 68 and the kids of the kids you teach have had kids, you might finally get to draw your pension. But, at the moment, you’re just a little more in debt every month and feeling as if something might snap. The long-term effects of these factors can take their toll.

Pull Factors

When I began my search, the jobs advertised for a classroom teacher position on the TES site, with no additional management responsibilities, would give me a net salary of around 30% more than my take-home, as an Upper Pay Scale teacher in England. On top of this, were end of salary bonuses worth around 4000GBP per year and accommodation allowances too. We would even be able to afford to come home and visit family each summer, as many jobs were providing an annual flight allowance for the employees. These perks were fairly typical of the jobs offered in schools in China, Malaysia, Thailand, etc. Some schools even offer to pauy for medical insurance and offer a 500GBP payment at the start of contract to buy homeware items. As my husband is also a teacher, we could double most of these benefits.

Location

It was clear that the schools offering the most competitive salaries and packages were where the most wealthy parents were likely to be located – the mega-cities. Sure, you can go and teach 15 minutes from the beach on a paradise island, but the salaries on offer are around half of what you’ll receive in major population hubs. As money was one of the major pull factors, we decided to apply for city jobs.

Childcare

During the initial application stage, it can be hard to gauge all of the expectations employers will have of your time. International Schools tend to have earlier start times and later finishing times than schools in England. There’s also the need to consider what you’ll do with your little ones during Parents' Evenings and late meetings, etc. as hanging around in mum or dad’s class might not be a desirable option for the school or for your offspring. When I worked in China, colleagues already in post helped me to find childcare in the form of an 'Ayi'. She was a dream – multilingual, kind, patient, an amazing cook...the list was endless. Her monthly rate cost me around one third of my salary and she was paid double February’s salary as it was Chinese New Year! There are countless agencies that can help you find help once you arrive. Things can be arranged quickly and so organising prior to arrival is usually unnessary and you are likely to want a trial before you commit to anything contractual. An expense, yes, but we really couldn't have survived those first two years without her.

Housing

As we had decided to move to Shanghai for our first post, we did need to consider our accommodation costs very carefully. In Shanghai, Beijing and Hong-Kong, accommodation costs are very high; especially if you want to live in something bigger than a shoebox. Most international schools offer a housing allowance, but usually they are not enough to cover a place for a family. The accommodation allowance our school offered would cover a tiny, one bed apartment. As a family, we were paying around 1300GBP per month for a three bed apartment, after our allowance was deducted. What’s more, the landlords often require a deposit of two months’ rent and the 1st rent payment up front. We needed a bank loan before we even stepped off the plane and started earning! In Bangkok, accommodation costs were much more manageable and comparable to most areas of the U.K. (outside of London.) There were also a range of accommodation types: from apartments to large detached houses, within budget. For 1000GBP per month, we were able to rent a three-bedroom house with a large garden and a swimming pool on the outskirts of the city.

Transport

Another factor to consider when choosing where to apply is how easy it will be to get around. In China for example, it is prohibitively difficult for many foreigners to get a driving license. In fact, none of the teachers I’ve ever worked with in China drove. However, the ride hailing services such as 'Didi' were reliable and affordable. Even when getting around with the whole family, there was an option to book bigger vehicles so having no car was actually quite liberating. For shorter trips, you can easily purchase and obtain a license for an electric scooter. At roughly 300GBP, you can get yourself a new one to whizz around the city at 30MPH…but do wear a helmet! In Thailand, we found it much easier to rent a car. As we lived on the outskirts of the city, this was convenient as ride hailing apps operated patchy service. The car was relatively expensive however, at around 440GBP per month, including insurance.

Preparing to go

  • VISAs

If you are satisfied that your new employer will offer a good salary, after living costs, and that they will offer a good standard of education to your children, it’s time to get organised. The school’s admin team should send you information about the VISA process and more recently, vaccine and quarantine requirements. Every International School I’ve worked with has been good at this but I’d still recommend asking questions if you’re unsure about something. Depending on the country, the VISA process might require multiple trips to the embassy. If you live a long way from the embassy, this can be a real problem. There are companies, who will, for a fee, sort this out for you. I’ve only used a VISA company once, but they were very efficient and took the majority of the hassle out of getting five of us to China. If you accepted an offer late in the academic year and there’s not much time before you leave (perhaps three or four months), this might be a good option for you.

  • Apps

If you’ve been ‘buddied up’ with a teacher at your new school before you go, ask them about Apps they recommend. Depending on where you’re going, there will be apps that everyone uses for communication, transport, banking, food delivery, and making payments and you’ll be using most of them the first day you arrive!

  • Shipping

The first two occasions that I travelled with my family, we did so with just a suitcase and carry on. This is very achievable and I the option I prefer. Shipping can be costly in terms of time and money and is generally worth avoiding if possible. Having said that, I have used a shipping company once for things like bicycles, my children’s books, sentimental things and a few small pieces of furniture. If you want to ship, get quotes from as many different companies as possible as their prices can really vary. As the items I shipped were inconvenient to replace rather than valuable, I opted out of the insurance which saved me about 20% of what the cost would have been.

Setting up

The pace of the changes in your life can be astonishing once you arrive in your new country. Typically, your employer might put you in contact with a local letting agent who will arrange for you see to a number of properties within a set budget. You are likely to be able to have picked the best of the bunch and moved in within about a week of landing. Before you sign though, see whether you can haggle a little with the price – it doesn’t hurt to try and also ask for any snag jobs to be fixed before you move in, e.g. replacing some worn fixtures or touching up the paint. I’ve found that my landlords are usually very happy to accommodate (pun intended)! The time between moving from a hotel to your new house or apartment might be so fast that you won’t have had time to buy sheets for the beds! This has happened to my family several times. We now pack a few old sheets and thin blankets just in case! Once in your new place, try doing a few dry runs of your commute. How long does it take to order a ride share, hail a cab, walk, cycle, scoot or drive to work? Get yourself sorted out with online grocery deliveries and a take-out service to make life easier and enjoy exploring your local area. If you’re looking for childcare, now’s the time to get in touch with people. I’ve been fortunate to employ people recommended by colleagues. Many childcare workers also know others in the same business so if a family at your school employs someone, ask them if they have contacts.

Potential problems

Problems you have with your employer when working abroad can be extremely stressful. Often it can seem as if school owners and the leaders they’ve employed to run their ship hold all the cards and that there’s little to no recourse for disputes. Should you find yourself in a situation where you have a problem with the school’s direction (or lack thereof) or a grievance with staff members, you’ll have to think carefully about the best course of action for you. I believe this needs stating as many dedicated teachers put the needs of their students first at all times despite the personal cost to themselves. Of course, everybody has their own boundaries when it comes to their relationship with an employer, however here’s my top seven list of poor behaviours from some of the International Schools I’ve worked:

  • Unqualified and/or poorly performing staff teaching dependents
  • Non-payment, or a change of medical insurance without consulting staff
  • Removal of end of contract flight allowance
  • Reducing the level of medical insurance without consultation
  • Reduction in pay
  • Swapping E-Learning periods for summer holidays without consultation, resulting in being unable to visit family
  • Regular late payment of salary (by up to 14 days)

No doubt this series of unfortunate events was confounded by Covid-19, but by no means were all of these problems experienced from wholly during this period and I’ve found that many colleagues in other establishments had experienced similar during their careers. The only recourse I felt I had when my contract was broken was to leave at a time that suited me rather than one that suited my employer. I remained very dedicated and professional and made an effort to be really cheerful toward my colleagues (after all my life would be easier if I could go with a good reference). Sometimes I had to wait longer than I wanted in order to ensure I received an end of contract bonus and other times I left my school with very little notice. You just need to weigh up what the best outcome for you will be.

Please don’t let my experiences frighten you though. I’ve found working abroad really rewarding both professionally and financially and I certainly don’t regret it at all!

Returning home

Deciding to return home can bring up a plethora of mixed emotions. Some of the very best teachers I’ve met while abroad were really looking forward to the rigor and professionalism that is characteristic of state schools in their home countries but were worried about the nosedive their personal finances would take. As I’d set out a strict saving plan, I was happy that I’d paid off money I’d borrowed for my wedding and had no remaining credit card debt…for the first time since I was 18! My husband had managed to clear his credit cards and even managed to pay off his student loan. As debt was a big cause of stress for my family, we felt, despite having limited savings, we were in a much better position to enjoy life in the U.K. My three children were also keen to go home – we’d promised them we’d settle in one place until they were done with school! They’d experienced different cultures, lived in some fantastic places and enjoyed holidays we could have never afforded if we’d had stayed at home. Once they’re grown, I may be tempted to try the international game once again.

Questions to ask

To be honest, there’s a laundry list of worthwhile questions to ask your potential employer before you travel abroad. My top three include:

  • Will you provide medical insurance for my dependents?

Schools really vary on this. In China, medical insurance which covers care at private hospitals, can be very costly, so some employers won’t cover insurance costs for kids. Annual insurance for each of my children cost around 60% of a month’s salary. The school paid the insurer up front and reclaimed the cost over a three-month period from my salary. The cost of comparable medical insurance in other areas of Asia can vary dramatically. When I worked in Thailand, the costs were relatively low so my contract also included full medical cover for my kids.

  • What does housing for a family of x people typically cost?

In my experience, the majority of expatriate teachers travel individually or as couples. Where companies offer accommodation payments as part of the contract, they may consider the price of a one bed apartment as the base point for their offer. When travelling as a teaching couple, some schools double the allowance, others just add a small percentage. There may well be a gulf between the cost of renting a one-bedroom apartment and housing that will fit a family so make enquiries before you go. I recommend asking your employer and looking at the websites of some letting agents in order to help you get your budget in order.

  • How is English, Maths, Science etc. taught in your school?

When visiting the websites of International Schools, many state that they follow the British National Curriculum, International Primary Curriculum, Cambridge Curriculum, Read, Write Inc, Whiterose, Rising Stars etc. If you have dependents that will attend the schools, I recommend doing a lot of digging! Some programs are very proscriptive and in depth, with precise objectives, lesson plans and resources. Others are less prescriptive, allowing good teachers to be more creative, and less skilled practitioners to make it up as they go along. If the people on your interview panel cannot talk knowledgably about their curriculum, that may be a cause for concern.
